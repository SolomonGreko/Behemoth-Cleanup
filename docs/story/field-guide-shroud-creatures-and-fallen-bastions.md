# Field Guide: Forms of the Shroud & The Fallen Bastions
## For the Tower Defense Game — Aethra / Vigil

---

## Narrative Element
Bestiary fragment and chronicle — naming the Shroud's creature-types and the four unnamed fallen bastions, with vignettes of how each bastion fell.

## Content

---

### The Fallen Bastions — Chronicles of the Lost Six

The ward-network of Myrrha anchored seven bastions across Aethra. Three have been named in the histories: **Keth's Gate** in the eastern Sunken Valleys (first to fall), **Myrrha** at the heartland (the great forge-city, fell in a single night), and **the Vigil** in the western plateau (last standing). The four that stood between them have been recorded only as absences — gaps in the chronicles where a name should be. I have walked their ruins. I have read what remains. They deserve to be remembered.

---

#### Haldrath, the Deep-Fortress *(Second Bastion — South)*

Built into a canyon where the river Ithin once ran, Haldrath was the bastion of silence before the Shroud ever touched it. Its wardstone was set in a cavern so deep that the Ward-Smiths' hammers echoed for a full minute after each strike. The garrison prided itself on its stillness — sentries who could hold a watch for sixteen hours without shifting weight, signal-bells tuned so low they were felt in the sternum before they were heard.

When the Shroud came, it did not breach the gates. It did not need to. It seeped through the canyon's natural acoustics — a frequency below sound that the garrison mistook for their own wardstone's hum. By the time anyone realised the resonance had changed, half the garrison had stopped speaking. Not from fear. They had simply nothing left to say. Haldrath was found with its gates open, its watchfires cold, its wardstone a perfect sphere of black glass.

The river Ithin has not been heard since.

---

#### Iron-Tine *(Third Bastion — North, foothills of the Frost-Rimmed Peaks)*

The northernmost bastion. The Ward-Smiths built Iron-Tine into the face of Mount Orynd itself, reasoning that the Shroud — a thing of stillness and silence — would struggle against the mountain's permanent howl. For three hundred years after the first wardstone cracked, Iron-Tine held. Its garrison developed a culture of noise: every hour, on the hour, every bell, horn, and voice in the fortress sounded together. They called it the Clamour-Rite. They believed it kept the silence at bay.

They were wrong, but not in the way you might think.

The Shroud did not silence Iron-Tine. It learned to *be* the noise. It hollowed the mountain's howl and wore it like a stolen coat. The last dispatch from Iron-Tine — received at the Vigil three days before the signal fires went dark — was not a written message. It was a recording, pressed into a resonance-crystal by the bastion's chronicler. When played, it sounds like wind through a high pass. Listen longer, and you can hear, underneath the wind, the exact moment the garrison realised they had been screaming the Shroud's voice for a hundred years.

---

#### The Weeping Manse *(Fourth Bastion — Northeast, the riverlands)*

The most beautiful of the bastions. The Ward-Smiths who built the Weeping Manse were not merely artificers but artists — they believed that beauty was a form of warding, that the Shroud's stillness could find no purchase in a place where every surface testified to life. The Manse was a fortress of gardens, of tiered fountains that sang in seven keys, of mosaic walls that shifted colour with the hour of the day. Pilgrims travelled from Myrrha just to stand in its courtyards and weep at the loveliness.

The Shroud took the Weeping Manse, and what it did there is recorded only in fragments — a chronicler's last entry, scratched into a fountain basin before silence took her:

*The fountains have stopped. Not frozen — stopped, as though water forgot how to fall. The mosaics are grey. I cannot remember the colour of my daughter's eyes.*

The Manse still stands. Its gardens are intact. Its fountains are poised mid-cascade, droplets suspended in the air like a held breath. Nothing decays there. Nothing lives. It is the Shroud's most perfect work — a place so beautiful it hurts to look upon, and so silent that even the memory of sound feels like a violation.

---

#### Tel-Kavros *(Fifth Bastion — Southwest, the salt-flats)*

Tel-Kavros was a military bastion — the largest garrison of the seven, a fortress built for war against a conventional enemy. Its Ward-Smiths specialised in weapon-forging, and its walls were ringed with constructs the old texts call *resonance-lances* — devices that could project the wardstone's frequency outward as a weapon. Tel-Kavros held for the longest of any bastion except the Vigil. It held through the fall of Haldrath. It held through the silence of Iron-Tine. It held as the Weeping Manse became a monument to stillness.

When it finally fell, it did not fall to silence. It fell to a thing the garrison had no name for — a creature the Shroud had spent five years building from the consumed will of every soldier Tel-Kavros had ever lost. The dispatches call it only *the Marrow-Wight of Tel-Kavros*. It walked through the main gate. The resonance-lances passed through it like light through water. It killed the garrison commander where he stood, and the wardstone shattered not from assault but from the sheer wrongness of what had entered its field.

Tel-Kavros taught the Vigil one lesson, and it is the only reason the Vigil still stands: *the Shroud learns. What worked yesterday will not work tomorrow. What killed Tel-Kavros is coming west, and it is not the same thing that killed Tel-Kavros.*

### Forms of the Shroud — A Bestiary Fragment

The Shroud unmakes. That is its sole nature. But it cannot create — only repurpose. Every creature the player faces was once something else: a person, an animal, a thing that had lived long enough to learn the shape of existence. The Shroud takes the vessel, empties it of memory and will, and fills it with stillness. What emerges is a hollow form driven by the Shroud's single imperative: to unmake.

Scholars of the Vigil, working from fragments of the Ward-Smiths' archives and the combat reports of six fallen bastions, have classified five recurring forms. The names below are a compromise — the Ward-Smiths had purer words, but they died with Myrrha. These are the names the Vigil's garrison uses, and they are sufficient.

---

#### Hollow Drifters *(Scout)*

> *"They come first. They always come first. You'll see them in the twilight — shapes that walk like people but hold no weight. Don't mistake them for scouts. They're not looking for anything. They're just walking, the way a door swings in a draft. The Shroud sends them ahead because they're cheap — a single night's silence over a farmstead yields a dozen. Kill them quick. They're nothing. That's the horror of it."*
> — Sergeant Ilyana Korr, Vigil Garrison, after her third night watch

**In-world identity:** The most common Shroud-form. Hollow Drifters are what remains when the Shroud consumes something quickly — a farmer caught in the fields, a traveller on the road, a sentry who nodded off at the wrong post. The consumption is incomplete. The body remembers how to walk, how to hold a shape, but nothing else. Drifters shamble forward with the muscle-memory of who they were, and sometimes, in the half-light before dawn, you can catch a flicker of the original — a gait you recognise, a tilt of the head. Do not look too long.

**Gameplay association:** The basic enemy. Wave 1 bread-and-butter. Individually weak, dangerous in numbers. Teaches core turret placement.

---

#### Still-Walkers *(Tank)*

> *"Something heavy. Something that resisted. You can tell — the Shroud had to work to hollow this one out, and it left the shell thick. A fortress guard, maybe. A Ward-Smith's construct. Something that was hard to kill the first time, and harder now. Aim for the joints. If it reaches the wall, it won't stop walking until the wall stops standing."*
> — Captain Verrus Aldaine, Vigil Engineering Corps, on the breach of Haldrath's outer gate

**In-world identity:** The Shroud's heavy infantry. Still-Walkers are formed from beings of exceptional physical density — fortress guards in full plate, Ward-Smith constructs of stone and resonance, creatures of the Frost-Rimmed Peaks that had evolved thick hides against the cold. The Shroud's consumption cannot fully dissolve this density, so it repurposes it. A Still-Walker is a walking siege engine, slow but nearly unstoppable.

**Gameplay association:** High-HP, low-speed enemy. Appears wave 2+. Demands focused fire or armour-piercing upgrades. Teaches the player that not all threats are solved by more turrets — some require better turrets.

---

#### Weeping-Throats *(Artillery)*

> *"Don't think of it as a ranged attacker. That's a category error. It's not attacking — it's expressing. The Shroud took something that could make sound — a singer, a crier, a mother who called her children home at dusk — and when it hollowed the vessel, the sound stayed. Only the sound is wrong now. It carries the silence instead of breaking it. Every note is an absence. Every cry is a little death. If you hear one, find it fast. Don't let it finish its song."*
> — Archivist Maren Sol, Vigil Lore-Keeper, notes on the Weeping Manse reports

**In-world identity:** The Shroud's ranged horror. Weeping-Throats are what happens when the Shroud consumes a being whose identity was entwined with sound — singers, musicians, town criers, parents of young children. The Shroud cannot digest the impulse to vocalise. Instead, it inverts it. A Weeping-Throat does not scream; it projects silence outward in concentrated form. The old chronicles call it *the distance between a note and its echo*. The Vigil's garrison calls it *something to kill before it opens its mouth*.

**Gameplay association:** Ranged enemy. High damage, moderate HP. Appears wave 4+. Must be prioritised. Teaches target prioritisation and turret range awareness.

---

#### Ash-Scuttle *(Crawler / Swarm)*

> *"Children. Most of them. The Shroud swept through a village and these were the ones too small to crystallise — no final spark, no Sorrowglass, just the hollow of a shape that barely had time to learn itself. They're fast. They're fragile. One of them isn't even a threat. But they never come as one. They move like spilled grain — a thousand tiny pieces, all flowing the same direction. And that's the thing about spilled grain: you can clean up a handful, but if you don't have a broom, the floor's still covered."*
> — Quartermaster Halen Dreth, Vigil Supply Corps, requisitioning additional mortar shells

**In-world identity:** The Shroud's swarm. Ash-Scuttles are the most pitiable of the Shroud's forms — they are what remains when the Shroud consumes beings too small, too young, or too transient to leave a Sorrowglass shard. Children. Small animals. Insects. The consumption is so complete that nothing remains but the impulse to move, and so the Shroud sends them in waves, counting on volume to achieve what individual weakness cannot.

**Gameplay association:** Swarm enemy. Waves 3/6/9/12/18... 3× normal count, 2.5× spawn rate. Individually one-shot kills. Rewards AoE investment (mortar). Teaches build diversification.

---

#### Marrow-Wights *(Boss)*

> *"Every fifth wave, something different comes. Bigger. Slower. Wronger. We call them Marrow-Wights because the old texts say the Shroud 'sucks the marrow from the world,' and these things feel like all that marrow pressed into a single shape. They were people, once — commanders, Ward-Smiths, heroes who held the line long enough to be noticed. The Shroud doesn't hollow them like the others. It savours them. Builds them carefully. Every Marrow-Wight is a monument to someone who fought back, and that's the cruelest part: the harder you resist, the worse the thing you become."*
> — Commander's Log, Vigil, entry appended by the previous Commander on the eve of their last watch

**In-world identity:** The Shroud's masterwork. Marrow-Wights are formed from beings of exceptional willpower — bastion commanders, Ward-Smith adepts, legendary soldiers. The Shroud cannot consume such will quickly. It must work slowly, over years, hollowing the vessel layer by layer while preserving its strength. The result is a creature of tremendous power that still carries echoes of the original. A Marrow-Wight formed from a bastion commander might still favour their old sword-arm. One formed from a Ward-Smith might instinctively target ward-frequencies. The Vigil's current Commander has standing orders: if they fall, the garrison is to destroy their body with resonance-fire before the Shroud can begin its work.

**Gameplay association:** Boss enemy. Every 5 waves. Massive HP, massive damage, large size. Gate-keeper fight. Teaches preparation, resource hoarding, and spike damage.

## Context

- **Location in game:** Codex / Archive menu entry; loading-screen fragment rotations; wave-intro narration hooks; tooltip expansions for enemy descriptions in pause-inspect mode.
- **Trigger condition:** 
  - Bastion lore: discoverable in the Codex from game start; specific bastion entries could be gated behind reaching certain wave milestones (wave 10 unlocks Haldrath, wave 20 Iron-Tine, etc. — creating narrative progression).
  - Enemy bestiary: each entry unlocks when the player first encounters that enemy type. Drifter entry on first wave. Still-Walker on first wave-2 encounter. Weeping-Throat on wave 4. Ash-Scuttle on wave 3. Marrow-Wight on wave 5.
- **Audience:** All players. The bestiary entries are written as in-world documents — a garrison field guide — so they serve both new players (learning what enemies do) and veterans (deepening emotional investment).
- **Tone register:** Hopeful melancholy. The garrison chronicler's voice — weary, precise, darkly humorous at times, never hopeless. The enemy descriptions are practical field guides with an undercurrent of grief.

## Output Format Notes

**For Hephaestus:** Enemy type keys in config.js (`scout`, `tank`, `artillery`, `crawler`, `boss`) remain unchanged for programmatic access. The lore names are for display: HUD inspect tooltips, wave-intro text ("The Hollow Drifters approach..."), Codex entries, loading-screen tips. Add a `loreName` field to the ENEMY config block mapping each key to its in-world name.

**For Aphrodite:** These names inform visual direction. Hollow Drifters should look like emptied people — humanoid shapes that seem slightly hollow, slightly wrong. Still-Walkers should suggest density and armour. Weeping-Throats need a visual cue that suggests sound/silence — perhaps a distortion effect, a mouth that is too large or too still. Ash-Scuttles should be small, numerous, pitiable. Marrow-Wights should be monumental, unique per boss encounter, with visual echoes of their original identity.

**For Athena:** The bastion-unlock gating (wave milestones tied to lore reveals) is a design suggestion. If adopted, the level threshold progression could have narrative companion entries.
