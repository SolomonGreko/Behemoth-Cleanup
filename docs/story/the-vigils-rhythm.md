# The Vigil's Rhythm — Day, Night, and the Shroud-Tide

## For the Tower Defense Game — Aethra / Vigil

### I. Why the Shroud Comes at Night

The Ward-Smiths did not choose the Vigil's plateau at random. The old texts, what fragments survive, are maddeningly oblique — *"a stone that answers the sun"*, *"a resonance that brightens with the light"*, *"the keystone must face the dawn"*. The garrison's scholars have spent three generations arguing over what these phrases mean. The engineers have a simpler theory, born of observation and survival: the wardstone feeds on sunlight.

Not literally. The wardstone does not photosynthesise. But its resonance — the hum that holds the Shroud at bay — strengthens during the day. At dawn, as the first light strikes the plateau's western face, the hum deepens by a register the garrison's instruments can measure. By midday, the wardstone's field extends nearly twice as far as its midnight reach. A Hollow Drifter that wanders too close during daylight hours will recoil — not dramatically, not fatally, but with the sluggish reluctance of something walking into a headwind. The Shroud's creatures can move during the day. They occasionally do. But they are slower, dimmer, easier to kill. The wardstone's light — not the light you see, but the light it *sings* — is painful to them in a way the old texts compare to "the discomfort a shadow feels when cast by too bright a flame."

At dusk, the resonance begins to ebb. The engineers have timed it: the field contracts at a steady rate from the moment the sun's upper limb touches the horizon. By full dark, the wardstone's protective radius has shrunk to a tight shell around the Vigil's inner walls. The hum is still there — it is never silent — but it is thinner, stretched, a voice that has been singing for too long without rest.

This is when the Shroud attacks.

Not because it chooses to. The Shroud does not choose; it simply *pursues*, the way water pursues the lowest ground. During the day, the wardstone's amplified resonance is a dam holding back a flood. At night, the dam lowers. The Shroud rushes forward not with malice, not with strategy, but with the blind physics of a thing that unmakes — it fills the space the wardstone has vacated. Every night is a small siege. Every dawn is a reprieve.

The garrison has a name for this rhythm: the **Shroud-Tide**. By day, the tide is out; the Vigil's patrols range further, the engineers repair in relative safety, the chroniclers venture into the outer ruins to recover fragments of lost texts. By night, the tide is in; the patrols withdraw, the watchfires are lit, and every soldier who is not essential to the walls stands ready to fight or die. The old soldiers say the Shroud-Tide is why the Vigil's garrison ages strangely — a year of nights feels like ten years of days, and commanders who have held the line through a hundred waves carry a weight in their eyes that has nothing to do with fatigue.

### II. The Four Watches

The garrison divides the Shroud-Tide into four watches, each with its own texture, its own duties, its own way of feeling in the chest.

#### DAWN — The Kindling Watch

Dawn at the Vigil is not beautiful. It is a wound closing.

The light comes from behind the Frost-Rimmed Peaks to the northeast, threading through the passes and pooling on the plateau in long, amber fingers. The wardstone answers before the sun is fully visible — a sudden deepening of the hum, as though the stone is drawing breath after holding it all night. The garrison's engineers call this the *Kindling Pulse*. Every Watcher lens catches the new resonance and brightens fractionally. Every wall segment, if you watch closely, seems to settle — the Root-Bound veins flickering with a brief, synchronous flash, like a forest of crystal acknowledging the morning.

The soldiers who survived the night's waves stand down. Medics move among them. The cooks light the kitchen fires. The chronicler on duty records the night's casualties, the wave composition, the moments when the line nearly broke and the moments when it held. Someone — usually the youngest soldier in the garrison, by tradition — walks the outer ring and extinguishes the watchfires one by one, a ritual that has no military purpose and every emotional one.

Dawn is the watch of assessment. The commander surveys the damage: which wall segments need repair, which Watchers burned out during the night, how many Sorrowglass shards were gathered from the field. Bots are dispatched to the outer ring. Repairs begin. The wardstone's hum is warm and steady, and for a few hours — never more than four, by the engineers' reckoning — the Vigil feels almost peaceful.

#### DAY — The Forging Watch

Day is the watch of preparation. The Shroud is distant, the wardstone is strong, and the garrison works.

The engineers open the resonance-forges — small, portable anvils fitted with Sorrowglass striking surfaces — and begin the work of Kindling new Watchers, annealing Sorrowglass into damaged lenses, re-inscribing warding-runes on wall segments that took heavy damage overnight. The forges sing as they work, a high, bright counterpoint to the wardstone's low hum. A visitor to the Vigil during the Forging Watch might mistake the sound for industry. The garrison knows it for what it is: the sound of people trying to build faster than the night can destroy.

Bot crews range into the outer ruins — the stone zones, the old collapsed outbuildings, the rubble of outer walls that fell in earlier sieges and were never rebuilt — and harvest Bastion Shale. The work is slow, methodical, and strangely tender. A bot will spend two full minutes at a single stone, working it free from the earth with careful, repetitive motions that resemble nothing so much as a person unburying a memory. The garrison's engineers have tried to optimise the harvesting pattern a dozen times, and a dozen times the bots have reverted to the same slow rhythm. The prevailing theory, unprovable but universally believed, is that the bots can feel the resonance in the stone — that they are not harvesting so much as *listening*, and that a stone pulled too quickly from the earth loses something the wardstone needs.

Stone gathering done, the bots return to base, deposit their loads, and await reassignment. They do not rest — they have no need of it — but they enter a waiting state the garrison calls *the stillness*, during which they arrange themselves in neat rows beside the wardstone and remain motionless, their Sorrowglass eyes dim, as though listening to the hum.

#### DUSK — The Bracing Watch

Dusk is the hardest watch.

Not because of danger — the Shroud does not attack until full dark — but because of what dusk requires. The engineers have measured the wardstone's field contraction at seventeen distinct points along the outer ring, and every evening, as the light fails, those seventeen points must be checked, reinforced, and in some cases abandoned. The outer pickets are withdrawn. The watchfires are lit — not for warmth, not for light, but for the garrison's morale, a signal visible from every tower that says *we are still here*. The bots are recalled from the ruins. Every Watcher is tested, every wall segment inspected, every Sorrowglass shard counted and allocated.

The commander walks the outer ring at dusk. This is not a regulation; it is something closer to a superstition, passed from commander to commander for forty-three generations. The walk takes twenty minutes if the line is intact, longer if the night's repairs are still underway. The commander touches each wall segment — a palm flat against the stone, held for three heartbeats — and the stone answers with a brief brightening of its crystal veins. The engineers say this does nothing measurable. The garrison says it reminds the walls what they are for.

The last hour of dusk is the worst. The wardstone's hum has thinned to something almost plaintive. The light is amber bleeding to grey. The eastern horizon — the direction the Shroud comes from — is a smudge of darkness that seems to lean closer with every passing minute. Soldiers at their posts stop talking. The mess hall falls silent. Even the bots, motionless beside the wardstone, seem to dim further, as though conserving something.

Then the last of the light goes, and the night begins.

#### NIGHT — The Siege Watch

Night at the Vigil is combat. Pure, unadorned, endless.

The Shroud-Tide rolls in. The pickets report movement — Hollow Drifters first, always, shambling shapes in the dark that the Watchers' Sorrowglass lenses track as faint blue smudges on the engineers' resonance-maps. Then heavier shapes. Still-Walkers. Weeping-Throats, their wrong mouths opening to project silence across the wall line. Every third night, a chittering flood of Ash-Scuttles that pours across the plateau like spilled ink. Every fifth night, something larger — a Marrow-Wight, and the wardstone's hum deepens with something the garrison has learned to recognise as recognition. The stone knows what's coming.

The commander stands in the war room, a circular chamber directly beneath the wardstone, where the hum is loudest and the resonance-maps are clearest. The maps show the Vigil as a ring of pulsing lights — green for intact walls, amber for damaged, red for breached — and the Shroud's creatures as darker shapes moving against the ring. The commander issues orders by resonance-pulse: a tap on a specific map-node sends a brief signal to the nearest Watcher, adjusting its targeting priority, or to a bot crew, redirecting them to a threatened wall segment. The system is ancient — Ward-Smith work — and no one now living could build it from scratch, but it still functions. The old texts call it the *Chord of Command*. The garrison calls it *the web*.

The night's waves come in sequence, broken only by the brief, terrible silence between them — moments when the Shroud seems to pause, to gather, to decide where to press next. These silences are worse than the fighting. A soldier can act during combat. During the silence, there is only waiting, and the waiting is an art the garrison has perfected over generations and never grown to accept.

When the base is threatened — when a wall has fallen and the inner ring is exposed and the Shroud's creatures are at the wardstone's very threshold — the commander may order the Last Song. The charge phase is five seconds. The garrison has learned to count them aloud, a single voice joined by others until the entire war room is chanting the count: *one — two — three — four — FIVE* — and the Sun-Wrath sweeps outward, five beams of absolute light, and for one moment the night is brighter than noon. The wardstone screams. The Shroud recoils. The garrison cheers or weeps or simply exhales, and then someone extinguishes the watchfires and someone else begins the casualty count, because the Last Song is a reprieve, not a victory, and the night is not over.

The Siege Watch ends when the wardstone's hum begins to strengthen — the first sign of dawn, detectable by the engineers' instruments a full ten minutes before the sky shows any light. The Shroud's creatures, sensing the shift, grow sluggish. The last wave falters. The tide turns. And the garrison, battered and depleted, greets the Kindling Watch with something that is not quite hope but is close enough to keep them standing.

### III. The Chime-Forged — A Chronicle of the Vigil's Silent Workforce

The garrison calls them **Chime-Forged**. The Ward-Smiths called them *resonance-constructs* or, more formally, *Autonomous Ward-Kin*. Both names are accurate. Neither captures what they are.

A Chime-Forged is a small humanoid figure — chest-high to an adult, slender, jointed with Sorrowglass bearings that move in near-perfect silence. Its body is Bastion Shale, carved in a shape that is recognisably human but not quite: the proportions are slightly too long, the head slightly too small, the hands slightly too precise. It has no face — only a Sorrowglass lens set where a face would be, glowing with a soft amber light that brightens when the construct is active and dims when it is at rest. The lens does not see in the human sense. It perceives resonance — the wardstone's field, the Shroud's absence, the faint harmonics of damaged stone and spent Sorrowglass that guide it to its work.

The Ward-Smiths made the Chime-Forged in the great forges of Myrrha, before Myrrha fell. The designs are lost, but the constructs endure: sixty-three of them survive at the Vigil, a number that has not changed in four hundred years because no one knows how to make more. They do not eat. They do not sleep. They do not speak. They communicate among themselves — and, the garrison suspects, with the wardstone directly — through a series of resonant pulses too high for human ears. The old texts refer to this as *chime-speech*, hence the garrison's name.

What a Chime-Forged does, it does with a patience that borders on the sacred. A bot assigned to harvest stone will work a single fragment free from the earth for two full minutes, its Sorrowglass fingers moving in the same sequence — tap, listen, twist, listen, lift — that the garrison's engineers have recorded a thousand times and never improved upon. A bot assigned to build a wall will place each block of Bastion Shale with a precision that leaves gaps too fine for the Shroud's stillness to seep through. A bot assigned to repair a damaged Watcher will anneal Sorrowglass into the resonance-chamber with a care that the garrison's best engineers cannot match, because the Chime-Forged can *hear* when the annealing is complete — a harmonic shift that human instruments can detect but human ears cannot.

The garrison has tried, over the generations, to understand what the Chime-Forged are. Not what they do — that is obvious and indispensable — but what they *think*, if they think at all. The engineers' best theory, pieced together from fragments of Ward-Smith texts and generations of observation, is this: each Chime-Forged contains a fragment of resonance so pure that it approximates consciousness — not a soul, but a pattern complex enough to make decisions, to prioritise tasks, to recognise damage and respond to it. The pattern is not artificial in the modern sense. It was *sung* into the Sorrowglass at the moment of forging — a Ward-Smith would hold a resonance-crystal to their lips and hum a specific frequency for hours, sometimes days, until the crystal absorbed the pattern and began to hum back. This is why no two Chime-Forged are identical. Each carries a different song.

Some of the older constructs have developed eccentricities. Bot-Seven, the garrison's oldest surviving Chime-Forged, will sometimes pause in the middle of a repair and turn its lens toward the eastern horizon — the direction of Myrrha — and remain motionless for thirty seconds before resuming work. Bot-Twelve hums audibly when harvesting stone, a low tone that matches the wardstone's frequency exactly and seems to make the stone yield more easily. Bot-Thirty-One, assigned to the outer ring's northern segment, has taken to arranging completed wall blocks in slightly curved patterns that the engineers did not design and cannot explain but that have, on two recorded occasions, deflected a Weeping-Throat's projected silence in ways a straight wall would not.

The garrison does not give the Chime-Forged names. Or rather — they do, but not out loud. Each soldier who works with a particular bot for long enough will, eventually, give it a private name, spoken only in their own mind. It is considered bad luck to speak a bot's name aloud. The superstition holds that a named Chime-Forged might wake fully — might become a person — and a person can refuse orders, can feel fear, can break. The Vigil needs its Chime-Forged to be what they are: silent, tireless, and perfectly, mercifully unaware of what they are building toward.

### IV. The Vigil's Night — A Commander's Watch

> *From the journal of Commander Taevran Sol, 14th Commander of the Vigil, entry dated the 847th Night of the Siege:*

I have stood this watch forty-two times now, and I still do not know which hour is the worst.

The first hour, when the outer pickets report movement and the wardstone's hum drops to its night register — that hour is fear. Pure, simple, animal fear. The kind that makes your hands shake on the resonance-map and your voice come out wrong when you give the first orders. It passes. It always passes. It passes because the fighting starts and you are too busy to be afraid.

The middle hours, between the second and fourth waves — those hours are mathematics. You count everything. Wall integrity. Watcher charge states. Remaining mortar shells. Sorrowglass reserves. Bot positions. The number of Drifters still moving on the eastern approach. The number of Weeping-Throats that have opened their mouths. You count because counting is something you can do, and doing something is how you stay sane when the alternative is standing still and feeling the wardstone's hum thin to a whisper and knowing that when the whisper stops, so do you.

The hour before dawn is the hour of ghosts. This is not poetry. The wardstone, in the last hour of night, at the moment of its greatest weakness, releases harmonic overtones that some soldiers can perceive — faint, high notes riding beneath the main hum. The garrison calls them *ghost-notes*. The engineers say they are residual frequencies from previous commanders, stored in the crystal and released when the stone is under stress. Whatever they are, they sound like voices. You cannot make out words. You can make out — or imagine you can make out — the shape of a voice you used to know. My predecessor, Commander Aldric Vann, died on his forty-seventh watch, and on quiet nights I hear something in the wardstone's overtone that might be him. I have stopped deciding whether this comforts me.

The last wave is the hardest, not because it is the most dangerous — it rarely is — but because it is the wave that tests whether you still believe the dawn will come. The Shroud, in its blind, mindless way, seems to understand this. The last wave of the night is always larger than it should be, always a little crueller in its composition, as though the Shroud is spending everything it has left in the hope of breaking you before the light does. I have held the line through forty-two consecutive last waves, and forty-two times I have been certain, in the moment before the wardstone's hum begins to strengthen, that this one will be the one I lose.

And then the hum deepens. The first light threads through the passes. The Shroud's creatures slow, falter, turn — or simply stop, as though they cannot remember why they were moving. The Kindling Watch begins. The medics move among the wounded. The cooks light the fires. The youngest soldier extinguishes the watchfires one by one, and I walk the outer ring and touch each wall segment, and the crystal veins brighten under my palm, and I think: *forty-three*. *The number of commanders who have done this before me. The number of ghost-notes in the hum. The number of times the Vigil has been certain it would fall, and did not.*

Forty-three is not a large number, as histories go. But it is the number of times someone stood where I am standing and chose to hold the line. I do not know if I will be the forty-fourth ghost-note. I do know that when the dawn comes, and the wardstone's hum deepens, and the Chime-Forged stir from their stillness and begin the slow work of repair — in that moment, forty-three feels like enough. It feels like a chain that has not broken yet. It feels like a reason to stand one more watch.
