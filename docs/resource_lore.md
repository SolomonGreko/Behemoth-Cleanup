# Resource Lore — Names, Tooltips, and Crystal Origin
## For the Tower Defense Game — Aethra / Vigil

---

## Narrative Element
Resource identity and worldbuilding — in-world names, HUD tooltip flavor text, and the origin lore for Crystal as enemy-drop resource.

## Content

---

### Resource Names & Tooltip Flavor Text

#### 1. Bastion Shale *(Stone — foundational building resource)*

**HUD Tooltip:**
> Quarried from the fallen bastions of the east. Each fragment was once part of a wall, a forge, a gate that held the Shroud at bay for a thousand years. The Ward-Smiths' craft still lingers in the grain — a stone that remembers what it was built to do. Heavy with history. Harder than hope.

**Design Notes:**
- *Tone:* Ancient, sorrowful, steadfast. The stone is a remnant of what was lost — grief transmuted into foundation.
- *Visual direction for Aphrodite:* Rough-hewn grey-brown blocks, faint moss or old mortar traces. Not polished; functional, weathered, monumental.
- *Gameplay association:* Building material. Towers, walls, fortifications.

---

#### 2. Sorrowglass Shards *(Crystal — rare enemy-drop resource)*

**HUD Tooltip:**
> The last defiance of a soul the Shroud could not fully unmake. Cold to the touch, sharp as a widow's keening. Each shard holds a single note of colour in a world the Shroud would drain to grey. The Ward-Smiths had a word for it — *keening-stone*. The soldiers who gather it from the field use no word at all.

**Design Notes:**
- *Tone:* Rare, dangerous, heartbreaking. This is the emotional core of the resource economy — every shard represents a person who resisted.
- *Visual direction for Aphrodite:* Sharp, irregular shards. Vary by colour — some clear as glass, some clouded, some faintly luminous. Always cold-looking. No two identical in a pile.
- *Gameplay association:* Rare drop from defeated enemies. Used for advanced crafting, ward-forging, weapon sharpening, resonance engines.

---

#### 3. Ward-Light *(Essence — mystical ambient resource)*

**HUD Tooltip:**
> The old resonance, made visible. It pools where the wardstone's song is strongest — a slow, breathing vapour that tastes of ozone and half-remembered music. The Ward-Smiths learned to distil it from the ley-convergences beneath the bastions. Without Ward-Light, no wardstone can be forged. No seal renewed. No binding held.

**Design Notes:**
- *Tone:* Mystical, ambient, life-giving. It is the breath of the world itself — the magic the Ward-Smiths worked with, now a resource the player must husband.
- *Visual direction for Aphrodite:* Soft, flowing vapour or liquid light. Amber-gold or pale blue-white. Gentle, not aggressive. Suggests warmth and potential.
- *Gameplay association:* Magical energy resource. Powers wardstone upgrades, resonance engines, advanced abilities, seal renewal rituals.

### Lore Fragment: Why Crystal Comes From Defeated Enemies

---

**The Origin of Sorrowglass**

The Shroud unmakes. That is its sole nature. Where it passes, colour bleeds to grey, sound thins to silence, and the living forget — first their names, then their loves, then that they ever were. The old chronicles call it a hunger, a stillness, a tide that never ebbs. They are all correct. They are all insufficient.

But negation is not absolute. The Ward-Smiths of Myrrha proved this when they bound the Shroud behind seven wardstones, a feat that required not merely power but a profound understanding of what the Shroud was — and what it was not. The Shroud unmakes, yes. But it cannot *create*. Everything it sends against the Vigil — the husks that shamble in stolen shapes, the void-beasts that move like wind across glass, the formless things that press against the outer pickets — everything was once something else. A soldier of Keth's Gate. A merchant of Myrrha. A child of Orynd. A bird. A fox. A stone that had lain in sunlight long enough to learn the shape of warmth.

The Shroud takes what is. It takes warmth, will, and memory. It hollows the vessel and fills it with stillness, repurposing the form for its endless siege. But it cannot take everything.

Some souls flare at the moment of consumption. It is not a conscious thing — not courage, not defiance in the common sense. It is a reflex, as involuntary as a gasp. At the instant the Shroud's silence closes around them, a final spark of selfhood ignites, and the Shroud's own crushing pressure crystallises that spark into something permanent.

The Ward-Smiths named it Sorrowglass. They had better words — *keening-stone*, *last-bright*, *the grief that does not fade*. The soldiers who gather it from the battlefield call it nothing at all. Superstition counsels silence. Holding a shard too long, they say, can make you dream in a stranger's memories.

Each shard is unique because each soul was. Some hum faintly, as though remembering a song that has not been sung since Myrrha's forges went cold. Some are warm to the touch, even hours after the battle. Some are clear as water. Others are clouded with what the old texts describe, with uncharacteristic imprecision, as *the colour of someone's last thought.*

The creatures of the Shroud do not carry Sorrowglass as treasure or as weapon. They carry it because they cannot digest it. It lodges in them — a splinter of what they were, a permanent irritation the Shroud's silence cannot dissolve. When the creature's borrowed form is broken, the Sorrowglass remains. It is the only part of them that the Shroud could not erase. It is the only proof that they were ever real.

We gather it. We use it. The Ward-Smiths' old formulae describe techniques for annealing Sorrowglass into alloy — metal that can cut Shroud-flesh where common steel passes through like smoke. The resonance engines of the Vigil burn brighter when fed a shard that still hums. And somewhere in the deepest archives, sealed behind doors that have not been opened since the Ward-Smiths walked these halls, there are designs for something larger — something that might require not one shard, not a hundred, but a city's worth of final sparks, bound together and turned against the silence itself.

Every shard you gather was once someone's child. Someone's love. Someone who held the line before you, in a bastion whose name is already fading from the world.

Gather them well, Commander. There is no other way.

## Context

- **Where these names appear:** HUD resource counters, crafting menus, upgrade screens, tutorial prompts, loading-screen tips, and any narrative references to resources.
- **When tooltips trigger:** On hover of the resource icons in the HUD. Short form (first sentence or two) for quick hover; full text available via a "details" expansion or a dedicated Lore Codex menu entry.
- **The Sorrowglass fragment** is a standalone lore entry, discoverable in-game — possibly via a Codex / Archive menu, or triggered when the player collects their first Sorrowglass shard (a brief narrative pop-up or journal entry).
- **Voice:** Mythic-elegiac, grief and defiance in equal measure. Consistent with the opening narration's register. Direct address to the player ("Commander") is used sparingly — only at key moments.

## Consistency Notes

- **Bastion Shale** connects directly to the fallen bastions named in the world lore — Keth's Gate, Myrrha, Orynd, and the unnamed others. The player is literally rebuilding with the ruins of what was lost.
- **Sorrowglass** extends the Shroud's established nature (anti-existence, unmaking, silence without malice) and gives it a vulnerability: it cannot fully erase identity. The "spark of selfhood" that crystallises under pressure is a core thematic statement — the world fights back not through strength, but through the simple fact of having existed.
- **Ward-Light** draws on the "peculiar resonance" of the Vigil's plateau and the wardstone's "old, slow song." It is the ambient magic implicit in the existing lore, now given a name and a tangible form.
- **The Crystal Lore Fragment** is designed as a mid-game discovery. It should be gated — not in the tutorial. The player's first few enemy kills drop Sorrowglass without explanation (an immediate UI prompt: "Sorrowglass Shard collected"), and the full lore is revealed later, when the narrative weight of "every shard was a person" can land properly.
- **No contradiction with existing canon.** The World Lore document establishes that the Shroud unmakes, that the Ward-Smiths knew secrets now lost, and that the Vigil is the final bastion. This resource lore builds directly on all three pillars.
