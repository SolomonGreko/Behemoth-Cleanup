## Narrative Element
World Lore — Aethra and the Shroud-War

## Content

### The World: Aethra

Aethra is a world that was once whole. Its lands stretched from the Sunken Valleys of Keth in the east to the Frost-Rimmed Peaks of Orynd in the north, and at its heart stood the great city of Myrrha, where the Ward-Smiths forged the seals that held the dark at bay.

The enemy is the Shroud — not a nation, not an army in the common sense, but a sentient corruption that predates memory. It does not conquer; it unmakes. Where the Shroud passes, colour bleeds to grey, sound flattens to silence, and the living forget they ever were. The old texts call it "the hunger that wears a stillness."

Ages ago, the Ward-Smiths of Myrrha bound the Shroud behind seven great wardstones, each anchored to a bastion built and garrisoned in perpetuity. For a thousand years, the wards held. Civilisations rose around the bastions. People forgot why the towers stood. They called them monuments, ruins of a forgotten age, and let the watchfires die.

Then the first wardstone cracked.

It began in the east — a low hum that curdled milk in the pail, then a stillness in the air that birds would not cross. The bastion at Keth's Gate fell silent. No messenger returned. No signal fire answered. When scouts finally rode out, they found the fortress standing, its stones intact, its banners still flying — and not a single soul within. The garrison had simply ceased. The wardstone at its centre was spider-webbed with fractures, weeping a light that hurt to look upon.

One by one, the wardstones failed. The histories record the fall of five bastions in a single generation. The Shroud advanced, swallowing the eastern kingdoms, then the north, then the heartland itself. Myrrha fell in a single night — not to siege, but to a silence that crept through the streets and left the great forges cold.

Now only one wardstone remains intact. Only one bastion still stands.

The Vigil.

It is not the largest fortress, nor the grandest. It was built on a high plateau in the western reaches, a place the old Ward-Smiths chose not for strategic value but for the peculiar resonance of the stone beneath it. The Vigil's wardstone is the anchor of the entire network — the keystone. If it falls, the binding fails entirely, and the Shroud will roll across what remains of Aethra like a tide that never ebbs.

The player commands the Vigil. Its garrison is the last army of the living. Its walls are the last line. There is no retreat, no reinforcement, no dawn to wait for — only the next wave, and the one after that, and the hope that somewhere in the old texts, in the forgotten forges, in the songs the Ward-Smiths left behind, there is a way to seal the Shroud forever.

### Opening Narration

*Fade in from black. The camera — or the player's view — settles on the Vigil at dusk. Distant fires smudge the eastern horizon. The narration is voice-over, delivered slowly, with weight. No music, only wind.*

---

They said the wards would hold forever. They say many things, at the end of an age.

I have walked the silent streets of Myrrha, where the forges burned so bright they turned night to noon. I have stood in the great hall at Keth's Gate and heard nothing but the wind working a loose shutter. I have read the last dispatch from Orynd — a single line, scrawled in ash: *The stillness is at the walls.*

Five bastions have fallen. Five wardstones shattered. Five garrisons erased so completely that even their names are beginning to slip from memory, like frost melting from glass.

Now the Shroud comes west.

What stands between the living and the silence is this place — the Vigil, last of the seven bastions, built on a plateau the Ward-Smiths chose for reasons they did not live to explain. The wardstone at its heart still burns. The walls still hold. The garrison still answers the bell.

You are its commander. You are the last general of a war that was lost a thousand years before you were born, and you will fight it anyway — not for glory, not for survival, but because someone must. Because the alternative is a silence so complete that even the memory of colour, of song, of love, would be as though it never was.

They will come in waves. They will come without warning, without parley, without end. The Shroud does not tire. It does not negotiate. It does not even hate you — it simply is, and always has been, and will be unless you stop it here.

The watchfires are lit. The walls are manned. The wardstone hums its old, slow song.

Stand your ground, Commander. Stand it well.

## Context
- **Where:** Opening cinematic / title screen transition. The narration plays over a slow pan across the Vigil's battlements, ending with the camera settling behind the player's command point.
- **When:** First launch, before the first wave spawns. Also available from the main menu as "Watch Opening."
- **Who:** A narrator whose voice has the weight of someone who has seen the fall firsthand — not omniscient, but witness. Could be the last chronicler, or the previous commander, or a voice from the wardstone itself.

## Consistency Notes
- The Seven Bastions: Keth's Gate (east, first to fall), then four unnamed others (open for future lore expansion), then Myrrha (the heartland), then the Vigil (west, the last).
- The Shroud's nature is anti-existence, not evil. This distinguishes it from generic fantasy dark lords. It is a force, a corruption, a stillness — not a personality.
- Ward-Smiths are a lost order of artificer-priests. Their technology (wardstones, forges, bastion architecture) is the game's magical framework. Future lore can expand on individual Ward-Smiths and their techniques.
- The "peculiar resonance" of the Vigil's plateau is deliberately left mysterious — a hook for mid-game narrative reveals.
- Tone: Mythic-elegiac. Grief and defiance in equal measure. Not grimdark hopelessness — the Vigil still stands, the wardstone still burns, and there is the hint of a path to victory ("a way to seal the Shroud forever").
- Player address is direct ("You are its commander") to create investment, but the narration otherwise maintains an epic register.
